"Kindness Can Change the World"

A model TED Talk script · LSC 1503 · Speaker: Layla · ~4 minutes
Hook
What if I told you that ten words saved my uncle's life?

Not medicine. Not a hospital. Not a doctor.
Ten words — from a person he had never met.

Pause. Look at the audience. Wait three seconds.
Personal Story
Three years ago, my uncle had a very difficult time. He lost his job after fifteen years. Every day, he sat in the same chair. He did not eat much. He did not talk. He just looked at the wall. We did not know how to help him.

One evening, he went alone to a small coffee shop near our house. He was very quiet — tired and sad. And then a man he had never seen before stopped at his table and said:

"You look like you are carrying the whole world today. I hope it gets lighter."

That was all. Ten words. The man walked away. But my uncle told me later — those words changed something inside him. He called me that night. He smiled for the first time in months. He started to talk to a doctor. He got a new job. He got better.

And I kept thinking: one act of kindness, from a stranger, in thirty seconds, changed everything.
Idea 1 · Kindness Is Good for Your Brain
My first idea is this: kindness is not just a nice feeling — it is powerful.

Scientists found that when we do something kind, our brain makes a special chemical. This chemical makes us feel safe, happy, and connected to other people. And here is the interesting part: the person who gives the kindness feels it too. Not just the person who gets it. Being kind is good for both people.
Idea 2 · Kindness Spreads
My second idea: kindness spreads — like water in a river.

Research shows that one kind act can make three, four, or five other people act kindly too — even people who were not part of the first moment. Think about the last time someone helped you, or said something nice to you. How did you feel after? Most of us want to do something kind for someone else too.

That man in the coffee shop probably forgot my uncle after five minutes. But my uncle now goes every Saturday to talk with other people who are having a hard time. One kind moment — hundreds of lives touched.
Idea 3 · Kindness Helps Us Feel We Are Not Alone
My third idea is the most important: kindness helps people feel they are not alone.

Today, life is very fast. We look at our phones more than we look at each other. We walk past many people every day and say nothing. But one of the biggest problems in the world right now is not money, and it is not sickness. It is loneliness — feeling that nobody sees you or cares about you.

Kindness is the answer. Not big, expensive kindness. I mean the small, simple things: using someone's name when you say hello, holding a door open, asking "are you okay?" and really listening to the answer. These small moments tell people: I see you. You are important.
Conclusion · Call to Action
So, I ask you one thing today — just one.

In the next twenty-four hours, do one kind thing for another person. Not because someone is watching. Not because you want to share it online. Do it because somewhere — in a coffee shop, in a lift, in a classroom like this one — someone is having the hardest day of their life.

And you have ten words. Use them.

The world does not need us to be perfect. It just needs us to be kind.
Section 01
The Hook
The Opening — Grab Them in 10 Seconds
~30 sec ▾
🎯 Hook Type: Rhetorical Question + Mystery
Walk to centre stage. Stand still. Make eye contact. Then speak slowly.
What if I told you that ten words saved my uncle's life?

Not medicine.
Not a hospital.
Not a doctor.

Ten words — from a person he had never met.
Pause for 3 full seconds. Let the silence work for you. Do not fill it.
⏱ Approx. 30 seconds spoken · ~45 words
💬 Rhetorical Question

Starting with "What if I told you…" immediately pulls the audience in. They start wondering: What? How? This curiosity keeps them listening.

🔢 Specific Number

"Ten words" is precise and intriguing. It's not "a few words" — it's ten. Numbers feel real. They create a promise to the audience.

🎭 Rule of Three (Negative)

"Not a doctor… Not surgery… Not a specialist." — Three negatives build tension before the reveal. The audience is now completely hooked.

⏸ The Power of Silence

Pausing after your hook is one of the most powerful tools a speaker has. It gives the audience time to feel the impact. Most beginners rush — don't.

Section 02
Personal Story
The Heart — Make Them Feel, Not Just Think
~70 sec ▾
❤️ Story Structure: Setup → Problem → Turning Point → Lesson
📍 Setup — Paint the Scene
Slow down. Speak quietly. This is a personal moment.
Three years ago, my uncle had a very difficult time. He lost his job — a job he had for fifteen years.

Every day, he sat in the same chair. He did not eat much. He did not talk. He just looked at the wall. We did not know how to help him.
🔁 Turning Point — The Unexpected Moment
Look up. Speak clearly. This is the key scene.
One evening, he went alone to a small coffee shop near our house. He was very quiet — tired and sad.

And then a man he had never seen before stopped at his table and said:

"You look like you are carrying the whole world today. I hope it gets lighter."
Pause again after the quote. Let the words land.
🌅 Resolution + Bridge to the Talk
That was all. Ten words. The man walked away.

But my uncle told me later — those words changed something inside him. He called me that night. He smiled for the first time in months. He started to talk to a doctor. He got a new job. He got better.

And I kept thinking: one act of kindness, from a stranger, in thirty seconds, changed everything.
⏱ Approx. 70 seconds spoken · ~155 words
🎬 Specific Details = Believability

"Fifteen years." "The same chair every day." Small, specific details make the story feel real. If you say "he was sad," people don't feel it. If you show it, they do.

🗣 Use Direct Speech

When you say the exact words someone said (like "You look like you are carrying the whole world…"), the audience feels like they are really there in the moment.

🌊 Show Feelings, Don't Just Say Them

Don't say "he felt better." Instead, show what happened: "He smiled for the first time in months." This is stronger and more interesting to listen to.

🔗 The Bridge Sentence

The highlighted sentence connects your story to your main ideas. It helps the audience understand why your story is important. Always bridge your story to your topic.

Section 03
Three Core Ideas
The Brain — Evidence, Logic, and Insight
~90 sec ▾
💡 Idea 1 · Say It → Prove It → Connect It
🔬 Kindness Is Good for Your Brain
Shift your weight. Move your body to show a new section is starting.
My first idea is this: kindness is not just a nice feeling — it is powerful.

Scientists found that when we do something kind, our brain makes a special chemical. This chemical makes us feel safe, happy, and close to other people.

The person who gets the kindness feels good. But here is the interesting part: the person who gives the kindness feels good too. Being kind is good for both people — and for your brain.
🌊 Idea 2 · Comparison + Evidence + Return to Story
💧 Kindness Spreads
Take one step to the side. A small move shows you are starting a new point.
My second idea: kindness spreads — like water in a river.

Research shows that one kind act can make three, four, or five other people act kindly too — even people who were not part of the first moment.

Think about the last time someone helped you, or said something nice. How did you feel? Most of us want to do something kind for someone else too.

That man in the coffee shop probably forgot my uncle after five minutes. But my uncle now goes every Saturday to talk with people who are having a hard time. One kind moment. Hundreds of lives.
🏘 Idea 3 · Big Problem + Simple Answer
🤝 Kindness Helps Us Feel We Are Not Alone
Look straight at the audience. Speak a little slower. This is your most important point.
My third idea is the most important: kindness helps people feel they are not alone.

Today, life moves very fast. We look at our phones more than we look at each other. We walk past many people every day and say nothing.

But one of the biggest problems in the world right now is not money, and it is not sickness. It is loneliness — feeling that nobody sees you or cares about you.

Kindness is the answer. Not big, expensive kindness. I mean small, simple things: using someone's name when you say hello, holding the door open, asking "are you okay?" and really listening to the answer.

These small moments tell people: I see you. You are important.
⏱ Approx. 90 seconds spoken · ~220 words
🏗 Say It → Prove It → Connect It

Every idea has three steps: (1) Say your point clearly. (2) Prove it with facts or a story. (3) Connect it to the audience's life. This makes your ideas easy to follow and remember.

🔬 Using Science Simply

You do not need to remember difficult words. "Scientists found…" or "Research shows…" is enough. The important thing is to explain the idea clearly in simple language.

💬 Include Your Audience

"Think about the last time someone helped you…" — This question makes the audience part of your talk. They are no longer just listening. They are thinking about their own life.

🎯 Why Three Ideas?

Three is the best number of main points. Two is not enough. Four or more is too many — people forget. Three ideas, clearly introduced ("My first…", "My second…", "My third…"), help the audience follow you.

🌍 Name a Real Problem

When you name a problem that many people know — like loneliness — the audience listens more carefully. It makes your talk feel important and real.

Section 04
Conclusion
The Ending — A Call That Echoes
~40 sec ▾
🎤 Technique: Call to Action + Emotional Final Statement
📣 Call to Action — One Clear Request
Return to centre. Stand still. Speak slower than you have at any point in the talk.
So, I ask you one thing today — just one thing.

In the next twenty-four hours, do one kind thing for another person.

Not because someone is watching. Not because you want to share it online.
Do it because somewhere — in a small coffee shop, in a lift, in a classroom like this one — someone is having the hardest day of their life.

And you have ten words. Use them.
💜 Emotional Closing Statement
Pause before this final line. Make it feel like a gift.
The world does not need us to be perfect.

It just needs us to be kind.
Nod gently. Step back. You are finished. Do not say "thank you" — let your words be the ending.
⏱ Approx. 40 seconds spoken · ~100 words
🎯 One Clear Action

The best calls to action are simple and possible. "One kind act in 24 hours" is something anyone can do. Do not say "change the world" — it is too big. Give people one small, clear step.

🔄 Go Back to the Hook

"You have ten words. Use them." — This brings back the "ten words" from the beginning of the talk. When you connect the end to the start, the talk feels complete and well-planned.

💎 The Last Line

"It just needs us to be kind." — Short. Simple. Easy to remember. A good last line is not long or difficult. It is a sentence that stays in people's minds after the talk.

🎭 How to Finish

After your last words, step back and nod. Do NOT say "thank you" or "that's the end." Let your words finish the talk. Silence after the last line is very powerful.

Section 05
Visual Plan
Images & Slides — What Your Audience Sees
6 slides ▾
The Most Important Rule for TED Talk Slides: Your slides are not your script. They show the feeling behind your words. Use one image per slide. Use very little text — or no text at all. The audience should be listening to you, not reading your screen.
🤝
Slide 1 · Hook
Two Hands — One Reaching Out
A warm, close-up photo of one hand gently touching another. No faces needed. The image should feel intimate and human.
Search: "helping hand warmth close up" on Unsplash or Pexels. Choose warm tones — amber, golden light.
☕
Slide 2 · Personal Story
An Empty Coffee Cup, Warm Light
A moody, warm café shot — a table with a cup, soft light in the background. It sets the scene without showing anyone's face.
Search: "coffee shop quiet warm light" on Unsplash. No logos. No text. Just atmosphere.
🧠
Slide 3 · Idea 1 (Science)
Simple Graphic: "Kindness → Oxytocin → Connection"
Create a simple 3-word flow diagram in Canva or PowerPoint. White text on a dark background. Nothing else.
This is the ONE slide where a little text works — because it IS the key data. Keep it to 5 words maximum.
🌊
Slide 4 · Idea 2 (Ripple)
Water Ripple, Top-Down View
An overhead photo of a single water droplet creating ripples. Symbolic. Instantly communicates the idea of kindness spreading outward.
Search: "water ripple top view" on Pexels. Make it full-screen with no text — you explain it with your voice.
🏘
Slide 5 · Idea 3 (Belonging)
A Circle of People — Community
An image of people sitting together, laughing or talking — seen from above or at a distance. Represents belonging and connection.
Avoid stock-photo-looking images. Choose candid, natural photos. Search: "community people together candid" on Unsplash.
💜
Slide 6 · Conclusion
One Line of Text on a Dark Slide
Your final slide shows only your closing statement: "The world just needs us to be kind." Large font. Centred. Nothing else.
Use a beautiful font (Playfair Display, Lora, or Georgia). Leave generous empty space around the text. Simplicity = power.

✍️ Now It's Your Turn — Key Reminders

Hook Start with a question, a surprising fact, a quote, or a micro-story. Never say "Good morning" or "My topic is…"
Personal Story Use your own experience. Specific details, real emotions, real dialogue. Audiences connect to people — not ideas.
Idea 1 State it clearly. Prove it (evidence, example, stat). Connect it to their life. Keep it to 30 seconds.
Idea 2 Signpost it: "My second idea…" Use an analogy — compare your idea to something they already know.
Idea 3 Make it feel like the most important one. Slow down. Look at the audience. This is your emotional peak.
Call to Action One clear, specific, achievable thing. Not "be a better person" — give them a real step they can take today.
Closing Line Write it first. Make it short. Make it quotable. Practise saying it until it feels completely natural.
Slides One image per slide. No bullet points. No paragraphs. Your voice carries the information — your slide carries the emotion.
